Background and Motivation¶

Held in England, Wimbledon is regarded as one of the most prestiguous and historic tennis championships along with the Australian Open, the French Open, and the United States Open. Wimbledon is played on a grass surface and is the oldest annual tennis tournament with centuries of tradition and history. Started in 2003 by Roger Federer, the recent tennis climate has been dominated by what is colloquially coined "The Big Four" players, which are Roger Federer, Rafeal Nadal, Novak Djokovic, and Andy Murray. The past 19 years of tennis were a heap of intense rivalry between these four players as they alternated taking the tile of tennis world champion. A overview of the history of Wimbledon can be found in this Wikipedia page.

This tennis status quo has changed on July 16, 2022, as Carlos Alcaraz defeated Novak Djokovic in an intense final that ended the streak of 19 years. It without a doubt that Carlos Alcaraz is incredible talent and is the future of tennis. However, I was surprised to see Djokovic lose on grass surface, which is supposedly his best surface. Watching this match, my family regarded it as "the end of an era" as the new generation of players take over the former generation of players.

However, was this truly the start of a new era of tennis or was this just a surprising upset. After all, the match was too close to decide, as the advantage seemed to alternate between both players. That question is what I want to investigate for this CMSC 320 final tutorial. In this tutorial, I will walk you through the entire data science pipeline to arrive at the insight to answer this question.

Interestingly enough, the first rematch between Carlos Alcaraz and Novak Djokovich after the championship is on the due date of this tutorial, which is August 20, 2023. Unfortunately, I plan to submit this assignment before the match takes place. However, in this tutorial, I will be constructing a predictive model to predict the outcome of the rematch. Join me and let us see whether the predictive model will be able to predict the winner correctly!

Explanation of Data¶

Split - Either the court surface or the type of tournament

M - # of matches played

W - # of wins

L - # of losses

Set W-L - Record of sets won and loss

Set% - % of sets won

Game W-L - Record of games won and loss

TB W-L - Record of tiebreaks won and loss

MS - # of matches won on straight sets

Hld% - % of service games held

Brk% - % of service games broken

A% - Ace Percentage

DF% - % of double faults

1stIn - % of first serves that were in

1st% - % of points won on first serve

2nd% - % of points won on second serve

SPW - Service points won %

RPW - Return points won %

TPW - Total points won %

DR - Dominance ratio calculated by (SPW / (100 - RPW)) and represents player's overall dominance

The next step of the data analysis process is to perform data cleaning and data tidying. We will be handling data by changing the structure into a format that is easier to perform operations and analysis.

Data Cleaning and Tidying¶

The first step we will be taking is to remove and drop the columns that will not be relevant for our purposes. In this case, there are many diffferent measurements of ELO. However, we will be mostly concerned with the official ELO given as well as rankings and points. As a result, we will be dropping the other forms of ELO measurement.

Furthermore, this has the benefit of ensuring that we will not have any NaN (not a number) values. This is because every player ranked pro will have an official ELO whereas they may not have the other form of ELO rating. We need to ensure that the rows we do need will not have empty or NaN (Not a number) values. When we are dropping the columns, we are also dropping many of the empty cells.

In the next step of data cleaning, we need to rename certain cells and change their data type to be numeric in order to allow it to be quantitatively analyzed and put into time series analysis. Ensuring that the date and year is properly formatted will allow allow visualization over time.

# Remove the % signs of the dataframe and convert it into a float
def convert_percentage_to_float(df):
    percentage_columns = ["Win%", "Set%", "Game%", "Brk%", "A%", "DF%", "TB%", "Hld%", "1stIn", "1st%", "2nd%", "SPW", "RPW", "TPW"]
    
    for col in percentage_columns:
        df[col] = df[col].str.rstrip("%").astype("float")
    
    return df

def get_outcome(player, row):
    if player in row["Outcome"] and row["Outcome"].find(player) < row["Outcome"].find("d."):
        return "Win"
    else:
        return "Lose"

# Apply the function to get if they win or lose
